The Dotcom Playbook for Crypto

We have one job left in this series: a practical way to tell the rare real crypto project from the roughly 95% that will be worth nothing. The good news is we don't need a crystal ball — we have a map, and it's already 25 years old. Hover or tap any underlined term.

1999, all over again

In 1999, the internet was clearly going to change the world. So money poured into anything with ".com" in the name. Hundreds of companies IPO'd on little more than a logo and a pitch deck. Most of them — Pets.com, Webvan, Pets.com's many cousins — went to zero within a couple of years.

But a few did not. Amazon survived the crash and became a giant. Google arrived a little later and reshaped the planet. The lesson is the one almost everyone gets backwards: the internet was not fake. Most internet stocks were just overpriced junk. Those are two completely different statements.

The whole frame in one sentence: blockchain is to crypto what the internet was to dotcom stocks. The technology is real and probably the future of how money and assets move — and most of the individual coins riding on it will still be worth roughly nothing in ten years. The skill is telling those two apart.

The playbook: 5 questions that separate signal from noise

When we look at any crypto project at the Lens, we judge it the way we'd judge any business: is it trustworthy, and is it genuinely needed? Here is how that breaks down into five plain questions.

1. Real users and real usage — not just price. The first question is never "is the price up?" It's "is anyone actually using this?" Real projects have people doing real things on them every day — sending payments, building apps, trading actual goods. If the only activity is people buying the token hoping to sell it higher, that's not a business. That's a greater-fool game.

2. Does it solve a real problem, better or cheaper? Amazon was genuinely better than driving to a store. Ask the same of any coin: does this do something people need better, faster, or cheaper than the old way? "It's on the blockchain" is not an answer. Plenty of things work fine in a normal database. The real ones replace a slow, expensive middleman with something clearly better.

3. Value capture — does it actually earn, or just sell tokens? A real business takes in more value than it gives away. Many crypto projects survive only by printing and selling new tokens — which quietly dilutes everyone who already holds them. Look at the tokenomics: does the project earn fees from real activity, or is it just funding itself by minting more coins?

4. Trust, track record, and transparency. Who is behind it? Is the code open and audited, or closed and unexplained? Has the team delivered before, or is it an anonymous founder and a wall of hype? This is the same question that gives a dollar its value: trust. No trust, no value — no matter how exciting the chart looks.

5. Survives a bust. This is the Amazon test. The dotcom crash wiped out the junk and left the real businesses standing — with their users intact. The crypto projects that matter are the ones that come out the other side of a brutal crash still being used. Hype evaporates in a downturn; genuine usefulness doesn't. A bear market is the most honest audit there is.

Red flags: the tells of the junk 95%

Read this twice. If you see these, walk away — they are the fingerprints of the projects that go to zero: promises of guaranteed returns (no honest investment guarantees anything); influencer pumping (paid hype dressed up as insight); "number go up" as the entire thesis (no use, no earnings, just momentum); anonymous teams who can vanish with the money; and tokenomics that mostly enrich insiders while regular buyers hold the bag.

None of these are subtle once you know to look for them. The hard part is emotional, not analytical: the junk projects are usually the loudest and the most exciting, precisely because hype is all they have. The real ones are often quietly busy building.

Problems are opportunities

Here's the optimistic flip side. Every weakness in this market is a business waiting to be built — that's the mindset we carry everywhere:

  • 95% is junk → opportunity: trustworthy ratings and plain-English screening tools that help normal people avoid the landmines (a big part of what we do).
  • Telling real from fake is hard → opportunity: the firm that builds an honest, transparent scorecard for projects earns deep trust.
  • Hype drowns out substance → opportunity: patient, evidence-first coverage that gets more valuable every time a hyped coin collapses.
  • Crashes scare people off → opportunity: whoever calmly explains which projects survived a bust, and why, becomes the trusted guide for the next cycle.

The honest bottom line

Even with this whole checklist, most projects will still fail. The dotcom era proved that even smart, careful people backed plenty of losers; the winners were rare and far from obvious at the time. So the realistic posture is humility: assume you'll be wrong often, size any position so a total loss wouldn't hurt you, and let the survivors prove themselves over years, not weeks. The checklist tilts the odds. It does not remove the risk.

Quick answers

If most crypto goes to zero, why bother at all? Because a few projects won't - just like a few 1999 dotcoms became giants. The point isn't to avoid the whole space; it's to apply a checklist so you're holding the rare real ones, not the loud junk, and to size positions assuming you'll still be wrong often.

What's the single fastest red flag? A promise of guaranteed returns. No honest investment guarantees anything. Pair that with anonymous founders and "number go up" as the only thesis, and you're almost certainly looking at one of the 95%.

How do I judge a project without being technical? Ask plain-business questions: Is anyone using it? Does it solve a real problem cheaper? Does it earn, or just sell tokens? Who's behind it, and is it open? Would it survive a crash? Trustworthy plus genuinely needed - that's the test.
