How it works

The full methodology.

Four rules govern every number in this track record. They haven't changed since the first signal was logged. These aren't retrospective — they're the same rules applied before each entry date.

Step 1 — Signal identification

9 sources monitored continuously

A signal fires when one or more of these sources moves in a direction that aligns with a sector thesis. Multiple sources pointing the same direction raises the conviction rating from 1 to 5 stars.

  • ‣ Insider buying (SEC Form 4 filings)
  • ‣ Congressional trades (STOCK Act disclosures)
  • ‣ COT positioning (CFTC weekly report)
  • ‣ Regulatory filings (DOE, NRC, EPA, DOD)
  • ‣ Government contracts (SAM.gov, USASpending)
  • ‣ Satellite & geospatial data
  • ‣ Patent filings (USPTO, WIPO)
  • ‣ Options flow (unusual volume, dark pools)
  • ‣ Dark pool prints (large block trades)
Step 2 — Exit strategy definition

Four strategies measured for every ticker

Every position is tracked under all four exit disciplines simultaneously from the signal entry date. The system auto-selects one as the recommended strategy based on sector and signal tier.

  • ‣ Buy & hold — hold until thesis is invalidated
  • ‣ Trailing stop 25% — exit if 25% below peak
  • ‣ Staged 2‑3‑4x — sell thirds at 2x, 3x, remainder at 4x or stop
  • ‣ Staged 2‑4‑8x — sell thirds at 2x, 4x, remainder at 8x or stop

Staged exits use a trailing stop on the remaining position after the first partial sale.

Step 3 — Strategy selection logic

Auto-selected by sector + signal tier

The system does not pick the best-looking strategy in hindsight. It applies a pre-defined ruleset that was set before the entry date and has not changed.

  • ‣ Structural mega-trends (uranium, AI infrastructure, gold) → buy & hold default
  • ‣ High-conviction speculative (hydrogen, cleantech, emerging tech) → staged exits
  • ‣ Volatile mid-conviction → trailing stop 25%
  • ‣ 4–5 star signals → eligible for 2‑4‑8x staging
  • ‣ 1–3 star signals → 2‑3‑4x or trailing stop
Step 4 — Backtesting methodology

13-year window, no backfitting

Every return in this table was calculated using these exact rules. No parameter was tuned after the fact to improve the numbers.

  • ‣ Window: 2013–2026 (13 years)
  • ‣ Entry: closing price on signal detection date
  • ‣ Exit: strategy rules applied from entry forward
  • ‣ Benchmark: SPY return over the identical holding period
  • ‣ All dividends and splits adjusted
  • ‣ No survivorship bias — losers are included
  • ‣ Returns are price returns, not total return (conservative)
Exit strategy comparison

What each strategy actually does.

The same position looks completely different depending on which exit you apply. Here's how all four work in practice — and what the 13-year averages look like.

Strategy 1

Buy & Hold

Hold until the fundamental thesis that triggered the signal is no longer valid. A regulatory reversal, a contract cancellation, a technology failure. Not a price decline — a thesis change. Best for structural mega-trends where the tailwind is measured in years, not months.

13-yr avg across all tickers: +344%
Strategy 2

Trailing Stop 25%

Exit if the position drops 25% from its peak price at any point after entry. Once the stop is set, it only moves up — never down. Converts PLUG's −90% buy-and-hold into +35%. Stops the bleed when a speculative thesis breaks before reaching the staged-exit price targets.

13-yr avg across all tickers: +198%
Strategy 3

Staged Exits 2‑3‑4x

Sell one third of the position at 2x entry price. Sell another third at 3x. Apply a 25% trailing stop to the remaining third. Captures the majority of gains on volatile names before they give back the rally. Recommended for high-conviction speculative positions with a 3‑5 year horizon.

13-yr avg across all tickers: +237%
Strategy 4

Staged Exits 2‑4‑8x

Sell one third at 2x, one third at 4x, trailing stop on the final third with an 8x target. Reserved for the highest-conviction signals on names with the widest potential upside. Requires more patience — the 4x and 8x targets are not always reached. But when they are, the result is significantly higher than any other strategy.

13-yr avg across all tickers: +255%
How the recommended strategy is selected
Structural — long-horizon
Buy & Hold

Uranium, gold, AI infrastructure, copper, defence — sectors where the macro tailwind is structural and measured in decades. The government data that triggered the signal (NRC licensing, DOE grants, Pentagon contracts) is unlikely to reverse. Exit only when the underlying data changes.

Speculative — event-driven
Staged Exits (2‑3‑4x or 2‑4‑8x)

Hydrogen, cleantech, emerging biotech, early-stage quantum — sectors with large binary outcomes. The upside is real but so is the downside. Staged exits lock in gains on the way up without forcing a full exit. The 2‑4‑8x tier is used for 4‑5 star signals where the upside case is significantly larger than 4x.

Volatile — mid-conviction
Trailing Stop 25%

Names that have clear upside but where the position sizing doesn't warrant a full staged approach. Or where the initial signal is strong but the thesis has more execution risk than a structural name. The 25% stop keeps the loss contained if the thesis breaks early, without sacrificing the upside if it works.

Special situations
Override rules

Occasionally a position reaches its staged-exit targets before the thesis runs its full course. In that case, the system flags it for manual review — the override can either re-enter at a new signal price, add a fresh buy-and-hold leg, or do nothing. These cases are noted in the signal ledger separately.

ENPH (Enphase Energy) was a solar inverter thesis — a component supplier for residential rooftop solar installations. The system classified it as a structural clean energy position, which defaulted to buy-and-hold: “hold until the thesis is invalidated.” The solar energy transition is real and ongoing — the thesis wasn’t wrong. But the stock was crushed by something the classification logic didn’t screen for: interest rate sensitivity.

Residential solar is financed through consumer loans. When the Fed hiked rates from near-zero to 5.25% between 2022 and 2023, monthly payments on a solar installation nearly doubled. Demand collapsed. ENPH’s revenue fell 50% in 18 months — not because solar lost, but because consumers couldn’t afford the financing. The thesis was structurally sound. The stock was rate-sensitive. The system didn’t distinguish between the two.

Was it a close call? No — and that’s the harder lesson. The system didn’t flag ENPH as borderline. It classified it cleanly as structural and moved on. A trailing stop would have exited at −25.8% when the rate cycle first started damaging fundamentals. Buy-and-hold rode it to −79.2% waiting for a thesis invalidation that, by the traditional definition, never came. The solar transition is still happening. The stock is still down 79%.

📚 What the system learned from ENPH
  • Rate sensitivity screen added. Structural classifications now include a secondary check: does this company’s revenue depend on consumer or project financing? If yes, it gets a trailing stop overlay regardless of thesis category — the “structural” label applies to the macro trend, not the stock’s tolerance for rate cycles.
  • Clean energy subsector split. “Clean energy” is no longer a single classification. Grid-scale (utility solar, wind farm developers, transmission infrastructure) stays buy-and-hold — it’s policy-backed, rate-insensitive, and driven by decade-long contracts. Residential solar and consumer-facing clean tech (ENPH, SEDG, etc.) is now classified as volatile/rate-sensitive → trailing stop or staged exits.
  • Valuation stress-test at entry. ENPH entered at ~80x earnings. The system now flags any structural buy-and-hold candidate trading above 50x earnings for a secondary review — high-multiple structural names carry rate compression risk that pure thesis analysis misses.
  • Thesis vs stock distinction. “The thesis is intact” no longer prevents a strategy override if the stock’s fundamentals have structurally deteriorated. A company can be operating in a growing sector and still be a broken stock. ENPH taught the system to separate the two.
